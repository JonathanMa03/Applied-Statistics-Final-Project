A Dataset Introduction

The dataset provides a comprehensive profile of secondary school students, capturing demographic, academic, family, and lifestyle information with the goal of understanding academic performance, particularly final grades in Math or Portuguese (G3). Students attend either the Gabriel Pereira (GP) or Mousinho da Silveira (MS) schools (school), and are identified by sex (male or female) and age (ranging from 15 to 22). Key family background variables include address (urban or rural), famsize, Pstatus (whether parents live together), and parental education levels (Medu, Fedu) and jobs (Mjob, Fjob). The dataset also notes the reason for school choice and the studentâs guardian.

Academic behavior is described through traveltime, studytime, and failures, while support and enrichment opportunities are recorded through schoolsup, famsup, paid, activities, and early childhood education (nursery). Aspirations for higher education (higher) and household internet access (internet) are also included. Social and behavioral factors such as romantic relationship status, family relationship quality (famrel), leisure time (freetime, goout), alcohol consumption (Dalc, Walc), health status, and absences provide further context.

Finally, student performance is tracked via three gradesâG1, G2, and G3âwith G3 serving as the target variable for performance analysis. This rich dataset allows for multifaceted exploration of factors influencing student outcomes

Predictors of Average Grades.

  • Does alcohol consumption have any predictive power over student average grades?
  • How does this differ by gender?

I am also interested in learning what other features may be important predictors of student grades. I am not going to attempt to predict student grade evolution over marking periods since all the features in the dataset (other than grades) remain constant over marking periods and are general descriptors of student backgrounds. This study investigates whether alcohol consumption meaningfully predicts student academic performance, and whether this relationship differs by gender. After merging math and Portuguese course data, we created a comprehensive dataset with average grades as the outcome variable.

A linear regression model using over 30 socioeconomic, academic, and behavioral covariates reveals that weekend alcohol consumption (Walc) is a statistically significant negative predictor of average grades among male students only, while weekday alcohol use (Dalc) does not show a significant effect for either gender. The inclusion of interaction terms between alcohol variables and gender significantly improves model fit, as confirmed by an ANOVA test (p = 0.00318), rejecting the null hypothesis that gender does not modify the effect of alcohol use on grades.

After controlling for 30+ socioeconomic, academic, and behavioral variables, weekend alcohol consumption (Walc) significantly predicts lower academic performance among male students, but not females. Weekday alcohol use (Dalc) showed no significant predictive power in either gender.

An important question to ask: Why are we testing both effects and interactions (X*Y=X+Y+XY) instead of (X:Y=XY)?

While individual effects of alcohol use (Dalc, Walc) and gender (sex) were not always statistically significant on their own, the interaction between weekend alcohol use and gender (Walc:sex) was highly significant. This indicates that the effect of alcohol on grades is conditional on gender â a classic case where interaction modeling is essential. Following statistical best practices, we retained main effects alongside interaction terms to ensure proper model specification and accurate interpretation of conditional relationships.

Results

Our findings show that the effect of alcohol consumption on academic performance is not uniform across genders. Specifically, weekend drinking negatively affects male studentsâ grades, while no such effect is observed for females. This suggests targeted interventions may be warranted for different groups.

Whatâs interesting about these results is that some features that would be conventionally thought as important did not end up in the top ten list (I would speculate that the variables such as Pstatus, famsupport, famrel, & absences are among those). The degree of fit is also quite low, those this is likely due to model misspecification, as there are so many factor and likert variables that regression trees might be a better fit.

Social research should prioritize causal inference, robustness, and effect size interpretation, not just variance explained. Endogeneity, omitted variables, and selection bias are more prevelant in observational studies.

Conclusions

  1. Does alcohol consumption have any predictive power over student average grades?

Yes â alcohol consumption does show some predictive power over student average grades, particularly weekend drinking (Walc). In a fully adjusted linear regression model that controls for over 30 covariates, higher weekend alcohol use was associated with lower average grades, although this effect was not significant, and either was Weekday drinking (Dalc), suggesting that the timing and context of alcohol use may matter more than frequency alone.

  1. How does this differ by gender?

The relationship between alcohol consumption and grades differs significantly by gender, particularly for weekend drinking. A statistically significant interaction was found between Walc and gender (sexM:Walc, p = 0.00179), indicating that weekend alcohol consumption negatively impacts male studentsâ grades more strongly than femalesâ. No such interaction was observed for weekday drinking (Dalc:sexM, p = 0.539), suggesting that this effect is specific to weekend behavior. An ANOVA comparison confirmed that adding these interaction terms significantly improved the model fit (p = 0.00318).
